Alex Rivera (Host) [0:00]
Welcome to The Work Revolution, the podcast where we explore the technologies and ideas reshaping how humans work. I'm Alex Rivera, and today we have a conversation I've been wanting to have for months. We're talking about AI failure — specifically, why 87% of enterprise AI projects never make it to production, and why a company called Go2 thinks they've cracked the code. My guest is Go2's founder, who's been on what I can only describe as a crusade against what they call "fantasy AI" — AI built on assumptions rather than reality.
Go2 Founder [0:42]
Thanks for having me, Alex. And "crusade" is a strong word, but honestly? It's not far off. I've watched companies burn through millions of dollars on AI projects that were doomed from day one — not because the AI wasn't good enough, but because nobody bothered to understand how the work actually happens before trying to automate it. That's the fundamental problem we're solving.
Alex Rivera [4:30]
Let's start with the big number. 87% failure rate. I've seen this stat cited everywhere, but what does it actually mean? What does AI "failure" look like in practice?
Go2 Founder [4:48]
It looks like a company spending $300,000 on a chatbot that handles 4% of customer inquiries. It looks like a "smart" document processing system that still needs a human to check every output. It looks like a recommendation engine that gets ignored because it doesn't understand the actual workflow. The AI technically works — it processes inputs, it produces outputs — but it doesn't solve the real problem because it was built on a fantasy version of how the business operates.

"Companies are building AI on a fantasy version of how their business operates. They interview managers, draw process diagrams, and then wonder why the AI doesn't work in practice."

— Go2 Founder, on the root cause of AI failure
🔗 Share this quote
Alex Rivera [6:15]
So the AI itself isn't the problem. The data it's built on is wrong?
Go2 Founder [6:22]
Exactly. Think about it this way. If I asked you to describe your morning routine, you'd give me a clean, logical sequence: wake up, shower, coffee, commute. But if I actually watched your morning, I'd see you check your phone in bed for 20 minutes, skip breakfast when you're running late, take calls while driving. The described version and the real version are completely different. Now multiply that gap by an entire organization, and you understand why AI fails.
Alex Rivera [11:15]
You mentioned consultants earlier. There's an entire industry — McKinsey, Deloitte, Accenture — built around AI transformation. What are they getting wrong?
Go2 Founder [11:32]
They're not stupid — they're just using an inherently flawed methodology. The consulting approach is: interview stakeholders, workshop with managers, draw process maps, then build AI on those maps. But here's the dirty secret of process mapping: what managers describe is the idealized process. The one in the handbook. The one they wish happened. Real work is messy. It has workarounds. It has tribal knowledge. It has "oh, Sarah knows you have to CC finance on those" and "the system crashes if you enter more than 50 line items so Tom splits invoices." No consultant captures that. Go2 does, because we watch the actual work happen.

"Real work has workarounds, tribal knowledge, and edge cases that no interview or workshop will ever surface. Go2 captures all of it because we watch work happen in real-time."

— Go2 Founder
🔗 Share this quote
Alex Rivera [18:00]
Let's get into the product. You call it the "observation gap." Can you explain what Go2 actually does?
Go2 Founder [18:15]
Sure. Go2 has three layers. Layer one is our desktop agent — it's called Cowork.ai. It sits on each worker's computer and observes how they actually work. Not what they type — we don't do keylogging. Not screenshots. We capture workflow metadata: which apps they use, in what sequence, for how long, how they switch between them. We see that Sarah processes invoices by copying data from an email in Outlook, pasting it into a spreadsheet, then manually entering it into NetSuite. We see the 14 steps, including the 3 weird workarounds she does for international vendors.
Go2 Founder [19:45]
Layer two is real-time coaching. As we watch people work, our AI notices patterns. "Hey, you've switched between Slack and your IDE 8 times in 12 minutes — your focus score drops 34% when that happens. Want to try a 25-minute focus block?" Or: "I noticed you copy data from emails to your CRM 47 times this week. Want me to handle that?" The coaching layer is both valuable on its own AND it generates the data we need for layer three.
Go2 Founder [21:00]
Layer three is AI agents. This is the payoff. After watching Sarah process 2,800 invoices over three months, we have a perfect model of how invoice processing actually works — including every edge case, every workaround, every decision point. We can deploy an AI agent that does it exactly the way Sarah does. Not a guess. Not a consultant's process map. The actual thing. And it runs in shadow mode first, processing invoices in parallel with humans, until it hits 99%+ accuracy. Then it goes live.
Alex Rivera [25:45]
OK, I have to ask the obvious question. A desktop agent that watches everything workers do — doesn't that feel like surveillance? How do you handle the privacy concerns?
Go2 Founder [26:02]
This is the most important question, and I'm glad you asked it directly. First: we don't capture content. No keystrokes, no screenshots, no screen recordings. We capture workflow metadata — app names, window titles, transition patterns, timing. We know you spent 20 minutes in Outlook, then 5 minutes in Excel, then 30 minutes in NetSuite. We don't know what you wrote in the email. Second: personal app categories — social media, banking, health apps — are excluded by default. Third: every employee gets their own dashboard showing exactly what Go2 captures about them. Full transparency. Fourth: we're SOC 2 Type II certified and GDPR compliant. The data belongs to the organization, and employees can request deletion at any time.

"We don't capture what you type. We capture how you work. There's a massive difference, and that difference is what makes Go2 ethical AND effective."

— Go2 Founder, on privacy

Alex Rivera [33:20]
Tell me about the coaching. What's an "aha" moment look like for a user?
Go2 Founder [33:35]
My favorite story is from a sales team. One of the reps — let's call him Marcus — was closing deals 40% faster than anyone else. Nobody knew why. Marcus himself couldn't articulate it. Go2 watched Marcus work for two weeks and found the pattern: Marcus always pulled up the prospect's recent social media posts before a call. He'd spend 3 minutes scanning LinkedIn and Twitter, then reference something personal in the first 30 seconds. "Hey, congrats on the marathon!" That's it. That tiny thing was his superpower. Go2 flagged it, we coached the whole team to do it, and the team's close rate went up 28%. That's not automation — that's amplification. Making everyone as good as your best person.
Alex Rivera [40:00]
Let's talk about the agents. You're taking human work and turning it into AI agents. Walk me through a real example.
Go2 Founder [40:12]
Sure. Acme Corp — a 50-person logistics company — had three people processing invoices. Go2 watched them for 90 days. We identified 14 steps in the process, including 3 workarounds for international vendors, an undocumented approval threshold that everyone just "knew," and a quirk where NetSuite requires a specific date format for European invoices. No consultant would have found all of that. We deployed an agent, ran it in shadow mode for 48 hours alongside the humans. It matched human decisions 99.7% of the time. The 0.3% were edge cases that got routed to a human review queue. Now it processes 847 invoices a day. The three people who used to do that? Two moved to strategic vendor negotiations, one became the quality reviewer for the AI agent. Nobody lost their job — they got better ones.
Alex Rivera [48:30]
Let's address the elephant in the room. Some people hear "AI that watches workers" and they think surveillance capitalism. They think "my boss is spying on me." How do you respond to that?
Go2 Founder [48:52]
I respond by pointing out that Go2 is fundamentally different from surveillance tools. Time Doctor, Hubstaff, Teramind — those are built around monitoring and control. They take screenshots, they track keystrokes, they generate "productivity scores" that managers use to punish. Go2 doesn't do any of that. We're built around coaching and capability. Think of us like a personal trainer versus a parole officer. A personal trainer watches how you move and says "adjust your form, here's a tip, you're getting stronger." A parole officer watches you to catch you doing something wrong. Same observation, completely different intent and outcome. Our coaching acceptance rate is 89% — people voluntarily take our advice because it actually helps them. That doesn't happen with surveillance.

"Think of Go2 like a personal trainer versus a parole officer. Same observation, completely different intent and outcome. Our 89% coaching acceptance rate proves the difference."

— Go2 Founder, on surveillance vs. coaching
