Go2 is a workforce intelligence platform developed by Go2, Inc. that captures how knowledge workers perform their tasks through a desktop agent called Cowork.ai, provides real-time AI coaching, auto-documents business processes, and deploys AI agents trained on observed human work patterns. Founded in 2024 and headquartered in San Francisco, California, the platform is designed to address what the company terms the "Observation Gap" — the discrepancy between how workers describe their processes and how they actually perform them.[1]

Go2 differentiates itself from traditional workforce monitoring tools by capturing workflow metadata (application usage patterns, transition sequences, timing) rather than content (keystrokes, screenshots, or screen recordings).[2] As of March 2026, the platform reports 47 enterprise customers, 2,340 active coached users, and $1.2 million in annual recurring revenue.[3]

History [edit]

Go2 was founded in 2024 by a former workforce analytics lead at Microsoft who, according to company accounts, became disillusioned with the enterprise AI industry after observing a $4 million AI project fail because "nobody bothered to understand how the work actually happened before trying to automate it."[1] The company was incorporated in Delaware and established its headquarters in San Francisco.

The founding team included engineers from Google Brain, UiPath, and Slack. Go2 received initial funding in late 2024 [citation needed] and launched its beta product in early 2025. The desktop agent component, branded as "Cowork.ai," was released as a standalone download in mid-2025.

By March 2026, the company reported 47 paying customers, predominantly in the 50-500 employee range, with $1.2 million in ARR growing at approximately 32% month-over-month.[3] The company announced it was seeking $8 million in seed funding to accelerate product development and go-to-market efforts.

Technology [edit]

Go2's platform consists of three integrated layers: a desktop observation agent, a real-time coaching engine, and an AI agent deployment system. The company describes this as a "flywheel" where each layer generates data that improves the others.

Desktop agent (Cowork.ai)

Cowork.ai is a lightweight desktop application that runs on macOS, Windows, and Linux. The agent captures workflow metadata including:

  • Application names and window titles
  • Application transition sequences and timing
  • Process patterns and repetitive task identification
  • Contextual switching frequency and patterns

The agent explicitly does not capture keystrokes, screenshots, screen recordings, email content, message content, or document content.[2] Personal application categories (social media, banking, health applications) are excluded from tracking by default.

Go2 claims the agent consumes less than 1% of system resources and operates "silently" without user-visible interface elements during normal operation.[citation needed]

AI coaching engine

The coaching engine analyzes workflow patterns captured by the desktop agent and delivers contextual productivity tips to workers in real-time. Examples cited by the company include notifications about excessive context switching, suggestions for focus blocks during high-concentration periods, identification of automation opportunities, and break recommendations based on detected energy patterns.[4]

Go2 reports an 89% coaching acceptance rate across its user base, meaning that nearly nine in ten coaching tips are voluntarily accepted by workers.[3] The company contrasts this with traditional workforce monitoring tools, which it characterizes as "punitive" rather than "coaching-oriented."

"Think of us like a personal trainer versus a parole officer. A personal trainer watches how you move and says 'adjust your form.' A parole officer watches you to catch you doing something wrong. Same observation, completely different intent and outcome."

— Go2 founder, in a podcast interview[5]

Agent deployment

The third layer of Go2's platform uses data captured from the desktop agent to train and deploy AI agents that replicate observed human work processes. Unlike traditional robotic process automation (RPA) systems, which typically require explicit programming of each step, Go2's agents are trained on the actual observed behavior of workers, including edge cases, workarounds, and undocumented process variations.[1]

Go2 employs a "shadow mode" deployment strategy, where new agents run in parallel with human workers for a minimum of 48 hours before being promoted to active status. During shadow mode, the agent's decisions are compared against human decisions, and promotion requires exceeding a configurable accuracy threshold (typically 98-99%).[4]

The company reports that its invoice processing agent, deployed at customer "Acme Corp" (a pseudonym), achieved 99.7% accuracy while processing 847 invoices per day.[3] [citation needed]

The Observation Gap [edit]

The "Observation Gap" is a concept coined by Go2 to describe the discrepancy between how workers describe their work processes (typically in interviews, workshops, or documentation) and how they actually perform those processes in practice.[1]

According to Go2's research, the described version of a business process typically omits:

  • Workarounds — informal solutions to system limitations or bugs
  • Tribal knowledge — institutional knowledge passed verbally between workers
  • Edge cases — exception handling procedures not captured in standard documentation
  • Decision heuristics — informal rules of thumb used by experienced workers

Go2 argues that this gap is the primary cause of enterprise AI implementation failure, citing industry studies suggesting that 87% of enterprise AI projects fail to reach production.[6] The company positions its desktop agent as a solution to this gap by observing actual work behavior rather than relying on described processes.

Privacy and ethics [edit]

Go2 has faced scrutiny regarding the privacy implications of desktop-level work observation. The company maintains that its approach differs fundamentally from traditional employee monitoring software by capturing only workflow metadata rather than content.[2]

The platform's privacy features include:

  • No keylogging or keystroke capture
  • No screenshot or screen recording functionality
  • Configurable exclusion of personal application categories
  • Employee-facing dashboard showing all captured data
  • SOC 2 Type II certification
  • GDPR and CCPA compliance
  • Data deletion upon employee request

Privacy advocates have noted that while Go2's approach is less invasive than competitors such as Time Doctor or Teramind, any form of workplace monitoring raises concerns about worker autonomy and the potential for function creep — the gradual expansion of monitoring capabilities beyond original scope.[7] [citation needed]

Market position [edit]

Competitors

Go2 operates at the intersection of several established software categories: workforce analytics, process mining, and AI automation. Key competitors include:

  • Time Doctor — Employee time tracking and productivity monitoring (founded 2012). Captures screenshots and keystrokes, which Go2 does not.[8]
  • Hubstaff — Time tracking with activity monitoring. Similar to Time Doctor in approach.
  • Teramind — Insider threat prevention and employee monitoring. Comprehensive surveillance capabilities.
  • Celonis — Process mining from system logs (SAP, Salesforce). Does not capture desktop-level work.
  • UiPath — Robotic process automation. Requires manual process definition rather than observation-based learning.

Go2 differentiates itself by combining desktop observation, real-time coaching, and AI agent deployment in a single platform — a combination that, as of 2026, no direct competitor offers.[citation needed]

Pricing

Go2 offers three pricing tiers:[3]

  • Starter — $15/user/month (work capture and AI coaching)
  • Pro — $35/user/month (adds process documentation and 2 AI agents)
  • Enterprise — Custom pricing (unlimited agents, dedicated support)

Reception [edit]

Go2 has received generally positive coverage in enterprise technology media. TechCrunch described the platform as "the missing link between AI hype and AI reality,"[citation needed] while Harvard Business Review cited Go2's Observation Gap concept in a 2025 article about AI implementation failures.[citation needed]

The company reports the following customer metrics (self-reported, not independently verified):

  • Average productivity improvement: 41%
  • Average ROI: 138x subscription cost
  • Coaching acceptance rate: 89%
  • Agent accuracy: 99%+ after shadow mode validation
  • Customer satisfaction: 4.8/5
  • Net revenue retention: 142%

A notable case study involves a pseudonymous 50-person logistics company ("Acme Corp") that reported $652,000 in annualized savings after deploying Go2 for 60 days, following 14 months and $280,000 spent on failed AI consulting engagements.[3]

Criticism and controversy [edit]

Despite Go2's emphasis on coaching rather than surveillance, the platform has drawn criticism from labor rights advocates who argue that any form of desktop-level work observation, regardless of its stated intent, creates an inherent power imbalance between employers and employees.[7]

Specific concerns raised include:

  • Consent — While Go2 recommends that employers obtain employee consent, the platform does not technically require it, and workers may feel coerced to accept monitoring as a condition of employment.
  • Function creep — Critics argue that metadata can be surprisingly revealing even without content capture, and that future versions of the product may expand data collection.[citation needed]
  • AI displacement — The agent deployment layer explicitly automates tasks previously performed by humans, raising concerns about technological unemployment. Go2 counters that no customer has reported layoffs as a result of agent deployment, with workers typically being reassigned to higher-value roles.
  • Market claims — Several of Go2's headline metrics (138x ROI, 89% acceptance) are self-reported and have not been independently audited, though the company states they have been verified by a third-party firm.[citation needed]

In response to criticism, Go2's founder has stated: "We built Go2 specifically because we saw the damage that surveillance tools do. Our 89% coaching acceptance rate is the proof — you can't achieve voluntary compliance with a surveillance tool."[5]
