A letter from the founder

"I managed nearly a million workers. Here's what nobody tells you about where time actually goes."

Dear fellow business owner,

I want to tell you something I've never said publicly. For seven years, I ran a staffing company. At our peak, we had deployed close to a million workers across hundreds of businesses. We were the people companies called when they needed bodies on the ground, fast.

And in those seven years, I learned something that changed how I think about work forever.

Here's what I thought the job would be: match workers with roles, collect margins, grow the book. Simple enough.

Here's what the job actually was: figuring out why productive hours kept disappearing.

Every client had the same complaint. "We're paying for 40 hours but only getting 28 hours of real output." And they always assumed the workers were the problem. Lazy. Distracted. Gaming the system.

But when I dug in -- and I mean really dug in, across thousands of placements, hundreds of companies, years of data -- I found something different. The workers weren't slacking. The workflows were broken.

People were spending 90 minutes a day navigating between software tools. They were doing the same task three different ways because nobody had documented the best way. They were waiting on approvals that sat in someone's inbox for hours. They were re-entering data from one system to another because nothing talked to anything else.

The waste wasn't laziness. It was friction. And nobody could see it because nobody was measuring the right things.

That realization kept me up at night. Not because of the staffing business -- that was fine. But because I knew every small business in the country had this exact same problem, and most of them couldn't even name it.

So I started building something. Quietly, at first. Just for our own placements.

The idea was simple: what if software could watch how work actually gets done -- every app, every workflow, every task -- and then coach people toward better habits? Not to spy on them. Not to punish them. To genuinely help them work smarter.

Think about it like this. If you hired a personal trainer, you wouldn't expect them to just yell at you for being slow. You'd expect them to watch your form, spot the inefficiencies, and say, "Hey, if you shift your grip here, you'll get twice the result." That's what I wanted for knowledge work.

We built it. We tested it across our workforce. And here's what happened:

  • Workers started finishing tasks faster -- not because they worked harder, but because the AI spotted workflow bottlenecks they couldn't see themselves
  • Teams that had been stuck on clunky processes for years suddenly had a clear picture of what "good" looked like
  • Managers stopped micromanaging because they finally had aggregated data they could trust -- not keystroke logs, not screenshots, just honest patterns

That project became Go2.

I know what you're thinking. "Great, another employee monitoring tool." I get it. The market is full of them. Screen recorders. Keystroke counters. Tools that take screenshots every five minutes so managers can play Big Brother.

That's not what this is. And I say that with conviction, because I've seen what surveillance does to a workforce. I managed nearly a million workers, remember. The minute people feel watched, they stop taking smart risks. They optimize for looking busy instead of being effective. They hide problems instead of raising them. You get compliance theater, not productivity.

Go2 is the opposite. The AI works directly with each worker -- privately. It sees their patterns, learns their workflows, and coaches them. "You spent 40 minutes on this report last week. Here's how teams that do it in 15 minutes approach it." Or: "You switch between these three apps 80 times a day. Here's a workflow that cuts that in half."

The worker gets better at their job. They feel supported, not surveilled.

Managers don't see individual activity. They see team-level insights: where workflows break down, which processes need fixing, where training would actually move the needle.

This is the part that matters most to me. Because the dirty secret of monitoring software is that it makes managers feel powerful but makes workers feel small. Go2 makes both sides smarter.

Let me tell you about the data, because it matters.

Over seven years of staffing operations, we built a dataset that covers the work patterns of nearly a million workers. That's not a marketing number -- it's the foundation everything is built on. When Go2's AI coaches someone, it's drawing from real patterns across real work, not theory. It knows what efficient looks like in accounting, in logistics, in customer service, in sales, in operations. Because it's seen all of it, at scale.

Most productivity tools are built by people who've never managed anyone. They're built by engineers who think the problem is tracking. It's not. The problem is understanding. Understanding where work flows and where it stalls. Understanding the difference between someone who's struggling with a bad process and someone who needs training. Understanding that a team's output dropped not because they got lazy, but because a software update changed their workflow and nobody noticed.

Go2 understands all of that. Because we lived it first.
