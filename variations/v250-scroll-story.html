Chapter Two
The Observation Gap

To understand why Go2 matters, you first need to understand why AI fails. And to understand why AI fails, you need to understand something called the Observation Gap — a term coined by Go2's founder that has since become a minor buzzword in enterprise technology circles.

The Observation Gap is the distance between how people describe their work and how they actually do their work. It sounds like a small distinction. It is, in practice, an enormous chasm.

The Process Mapping Problem

Consider how a typical AI implementation begins. A company decides to automate invoice processing. They hire a consulting firm. The consultants interview the accounts payable team: "Walk us through how you process an invoice." The AP team describes a clean, seven-step process. The consultants draw a flowchart. The AI engineers build a system that follows the flowchart.

Then it goes live, and chaos ensues.

What Consultants Document

A clean 7-step process: Receive invoice. Verify PO match. Enter data. Route for approval. Process payment. File record. Done. Estimated time: 4 minutes per invoice.

What Go2 Observes: Step 1

Sarah opens the email in Outlook. But she doesn't just read it — she checks the sender against a mental list of "problem vendors" she's built over 3 years. If it's Vendor Alpha, she immediately opens a separate spreadsheet to check for the recurring billing error they've had since 2023.

What Go2 Observes: Step 2

For international vendors, Sarah copies the invoice total and pastes it into a currency converter — not the one in NetSuite (which is "always wrong by a day" according to her), but a specific bookmark she trusts. She then manually adjusts the exchange rate in the system. This undocumented step affects 23% of invoices.

What Go2 Observes: Step 3

When an invoice has more than 50 line items, NetSuite crashes. Sarah discovered this in 2024 and developed a workaround: she splits the invoice into two entries, processes them separately, then manually links them with a note. Nobody else knows about this. It isn't in any documentation.

The 14-Step Reality

What consultants documented as 7 steps is actually 14 — including 3 critical workarounds, 2 undocumented approval thresholds, and a date format quirk that only applies to European vendors. Go2 captured all of this automatically by watching Sarah work for 90 days.

This is the Observation Gap in action. The described process is a seven-step flowchart. The real process is a fourteen-step maze of edge cases, workarounds, tribal knowledge, and institutional memory that exists nowhere except in the heads of the people who do the work every day.

"When you build AI on the described process, you get a system that works perfectly on the 60% of invoices that are straightforward and fails catastrophically on the 40% that aren't. That's why AI 'almost works' at so many companies — it handles the easy stuff and chokes on anything that requires the human judgment and institutional knowledge that nobody thought to capture."— Go2 Founder
Chapter Three
The Desktop Agent Revolution

Go2's solution begins with Cowork.ai, a desktop agent that silently observes how people actually work. The word "silently" is important here, because the first question everyone asks about a tool that watches workers is the obvious one: isn't this just surveillance?

The answer, according to Go2, is an emphatic no — and the distinction matters enormously.

What Cowork.ai Captures

Workflow metadata: which applications are used, in what sequence, for how long, and how workers transition between them. It sees that Sarah spent 3 minutes in Outlook, then 2 minutes in Excel, then 8 minutes in NetSuite. It tracks the pattern of application switches that constitute a "process."

What Cowork.ai Does NOT Capture

Keystrokes. Screen content. Screenshots. Emails. Messages. Documents. The content of work is invisible to Go2. Only the shape of work — the pattern, the rhythm, the flow — is captured.

Chapter Four
The Coaching Layer

The second layer of Go2 is where the product transitions from interesting to potentially transformative. While the desktop agent captures work patterns, Go2's AI coaching engine analyzes those patterns in real-time and delivers personalized productivity tips to workers as they work.

This is not the ham-fisted "you've been idle for 5 minutes" approach of legacy monitoring tools. Go2's coaching is contextual, specific, and — critically — helpful enough that 89% of workers voluntarily accept the suggestions.

"Think of us like a personal trainer versus a parole officer. A personal trainer watches how you move and says 'adjust your form, here's a tip, you're getting stronger.' A parole officer watches you to catch you doing something wrong. Same observation, completely different intent and outcome."— Go2 Founder

The coaching moments are remarkably specific. Go2 doesn't just say "take a break" — it says "your typing speed has dropped 23% in the last 30 minutes, which historically correlates with a 15% productivity dip in your afternoon. A 5-minute break now would likely boost your remaining output by 18%." The specificity matters because it transforms the tip from an annoyance into genuinely useful intelligence.

The Marcus Johnson Story

Perhaps the most compelling coaching story involves a sales team. One rep — whom Go2 calls Marcus — consistently closed deals 40% faster than his peers. Nobody, including Marcus himself, could articulate why. Go2 watched Marcus work for two weeks and identified the pattern: before every sales call, Marcus spent exactly 3 minutes scanning the prospect's LinkedIn and Twitter. He'd then open the call with a personal reference — "Congratulations on the marathon!" or "I saw your post about the new office."

Go2 flagged the pattern, coached the entire team to adopt it, and the team's close rate improved 28% within a month. Not through automation — through amplification. Making everyone as good as the best person.

Chapter Five
From Human to Agent

The third layer is Go2's endgame, and it's where the product's full strategic vision becomes clear. After weeks or months of observing how workers perform a process — including every edge case, every workaround, every piece of tribal knowledge — Go2 can deploy an AI agent that replicates the process with near-perfect fidelity.

These are not generic AI agents trained on theoretical process maps. They are agents trained on the actual observed behavior of the humans who do the work best.

The Journey From Observation to Agent

Day 1-7: Observation

Desktop agent begins capturing Sarah's invoice processing workflow. Identifies 14 steps, 3 workarounds, 2 edge cases.

Day 8-30: Pattern Recognition

Go2's AI identifies consistent patterns across 200+ observed invoice processing sessions. Maps decision trees for vendor types, amounts, and exceptions.

Day 31-60: Coaching

Real-time coaching begins. Sarah receives tips that improve her own process. Each coaching interaction further refines Go2's understanding of the work.

Day 61-90: Model Building

Go2 builds a complete decision model based on 2,847 observed invoice processing sessions. Includes handling for the NetSuite 50-line-item bug, international currency quirks, and vendor-specific rules.

Day 91-92: Shadow Mode

AI agent deployed in shadow mode — processes invoices in parallel with humans without taking action. Achieves 99.2% agreement with human decisions on first run.

Day 93+: Live

Agent promoted to active. Processes 847 invoices/day at 99.7% accuracy. Low-confidence cases routed to human review queue. Sarah moves to vendor strategy.

The safety mechanism — shadow mode — is worth examining. Before any Go2 agent goes live, it runs in parallel with human workers, processing the same tasks simultaneously. The agent's decisions are compared against the human's decisions. Only when the agreement rate exceeds a threshold (typically 98-99%) does the agent get promoted to active status.

Even in active mode, any decision where the agent's confidence falls below a configurable threshold gets automatically routed to a human review queue. There is no fully autonomous mode. Go2 agents always operate with a human safety net.

Chapter Seven
The Questions That Remain

Go2's story is compelling, but it's also early. The company has 47 customers and $1.2 million in annual recurring revenue — impressive for a startup, but a rounding error in the enterprise software market. The questions that will determine whether Go2 becomes a footnote or a foundational technology are still being answered.

Can the approach scale beyond the 50-500 employee sweet spot? Can Go2 compete if the big players — Microsoft, Google, Salesforce — decide to build similar capabilities? And can a company that watches how people work maintain the trust of those people as it grows?

These are fair questions, and Go2's answers remain to be proven at scale. What is not in question is the diagnosis: the AI industry has an observation problem, and most of the $200 billion in failed AI spending can be traced back to a single, fixable error — building AI on how people describe their work rather than how they actually do it.

Whether Go2 is the company that definitively solves this problem, they have undeniably identified it. And in the process, they've built something that a growing number of companies are finding genuinely, measurably, provably useful.

For Maria Torres, that's enough.
