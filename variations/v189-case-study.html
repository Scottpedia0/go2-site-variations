Section I

The Problem: A $340 Billion Blind Spot

The enterprise AI market is projected to exceed $340 billion by 2027. Yet the vast majority of AI initiatives fail to deliver measurable business value. This is not a matter of hype versus reality -- the technology works. The failure is structural.

Two landmark studies frame the scope of the crisis. MIT's research found that 95% of AI pilot projects fail to reach production deployment. Separately, Boston Consulting Group's analysis revealed that only 5% of companies generate meaningful value from their AI investments. The convergence of these findings points to a systemic problem.

The question, then, is not whether AI works. It does. The question is why organizations cannot make it work for them. Our analysis points to a single, systemic cause: companies lack data about how work actually happens.

"Organizations are deploying AI into a context vacuum. They have powerful general-purpose models with zero knowledge of their specific processes, workflows, or institutional knowledge." -- Go2 Research Analysis, 2026
Section II

The Insight: The Data Layer That Doesn't Exist

Consider the analogy of GPS navigation. Satellite positioning technology existed for decades before it became useful to consumers. What was missing was not the technology -- it was the map data. Without comprehensive, real-time road data, GPS was a brilliant capability with no practical application.

The same dynamic is at play in enterprise AI. Organizations have purchased seats for ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, and dozens of other AI tools. These are, objectively, remarkable general-purpose intelligences. But they operate without any understanding of how the specific organization works -- its processes, its tribal knowledge, its edge cases, its actual workflows.

The Sidecar Hypothesis

Go2's insight was that the solution is not to build another AI tool, but to build the infrastructure layer that makes existing AI tools useful. Rather than replacing Claude or ChatGPT, Go2 sidecars them -- providing the organizational context they are missing.

Section III

The Solution: Cowork.ai and the Go2 Infrastructure

Go2's approach centers on a lightweight desktop agent called Cowork.ai, deployed alongside every worker in the organization. Critically, Cowork.ai is designed as a coaching tool, not a monitoring tool -- a distinction that has proven essential for adoption and worker satisfaction.

How it works

1. Workflow capture. Cowork.ai observes how work actually happens -- not keystrokes or screenshots, but process patterns, workflow sequences, and decision points. It builds a living map of institutional knowledge.

2. Real-time coaching. Workers receive contextual nudges, auto-documented processes, and personalized productivity tips. The system identifies best practices and gently guides workers toward them.

3. AI context injection. The captured workflow data feeds directly into existing AI tools. Claude, ChatGPT, and other models suddenly understand the organization's specific processes, enabling dramatically more useful outputs.

4. Automation surface discovery. With comprehensive data about how work happens, Go2 continuously identifies automation opportunities and provides the training data needed to build them.

The Waze Analogy Go2 operates on a collective intelligence model analogous to Waze. Individual workers contribute workflow data (anonymized and aggregated), and the entire network benefits from the resulting intelligence. With 2M+ worker profiles, pattern recognition across industries and functions creates a compound advantage that improves continuously.

Conclusion

Implications for Enterprise AI Strategy

The evidence suggests that the AI implementation gap is not primarily a technology problem, a talent problem, or a strategy problem. It is an infrastructure problem. Organizations have been attempting to deploy AI without the foundational data layer that makes AI useful in their specific context.

Go2 represents an emerging category of AI infrastructure -- tools that don't compete with AI models but make them functional within organizations. As the enterprise AI market continues to grow, the infrastructure layer may prove more valuable than the models themselves.

For organizations currently struggling with AI adoption -- which is to say, most organizations -- the implication is clear: stop investing in more AI tools and start investing in the data infrastructure that makes your existing tools work.
