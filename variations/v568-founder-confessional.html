I Ran a 50-Person Company Completely Blind for 18 Months

A founder's confession about what happens when you scale past the point where you can see what's happening — and what it almost cost me.

When we were 8 people, I knew everything.

I knew Sarah worked best before 10 AM. I knew Marcus needed two hours of uninterrupted time after lunch to do his best engineering work. I knew that Thursday afternoons were when the sales team made their best calls, and that pulling anyone into a meeting during those hours was a productivity crime.

I didn't need a dashboard. I didn't need analytics. I felt the rhythm of my company because I was in it, surrounded by it, part of it.

Then we raised our Series A. And everything changed.

...

We grew from 8 to 50 in eleven months. Every week, new faces. New teams. New managers who'd never managed before. New processes bolted onto old ones. The company I'd built by hand was becoming something I couldn't hold in my head anymore.

The first thing I lost was timing. I used to know when people were in flow state. Now I'd walk through an office of people I half-recognized, and I had no idea if they were deep in work or deep in Slack.

The second thing I lost was intuition. At 8 people, a bad week shows up as a vibe. At 50, it shows up as a missed quarterly target three months later.

The third thing I lost was trust — in myself. I couldn't tell if we were productive or just busy. If our engineers were building or just meeting. If our sales team was closing or just calling.

"I was running a company by feel in a world that required sight. And I was too proud to admit I'd gone blind."

I did what most founders do: I overcompensated.

More meetings. Weekly all-hands. Monday standups. Tuesday syncs. Wednesday check-ins. Thursday status updates. Friday retrospectives. I was filling the visibility gap with meetings, and the meetings were creating the productivity gap.

31 hours/month
Average time per employee spent in meetings I'd created to "stay aligned." I was the bottleneck I was trying to prevent.

One night — it was a Tuesday, I remember because I'd just come from our third "alignment" meeting of the day — I opened my laptop and found three resignation letters. Three. In one day.

All senior. All people I thought were happy. All people who'd been with us from the early days.

The exit interviews were brutal. Not because they were angry. Because they were sad.

"I loved this place when it was small. Now I spend more time explaining my work than doing it."

"You used to protect our focus time. Now you're the biggest interruption."

"I don't know what my job is anymore. I just know it's not what I was hired to do."

That was the worst night of my professional life.

I almost bought a surveillance tool. I'm not proud of this. I was so desperate for visibility that I almost installed software that screenshots employee screens every 5 minutes. I had the contract ready to sign.

Then I imagined telling Sarah — the first employee who believed in this company — that I'd be watching her screen. And I closed the laptop.

...
