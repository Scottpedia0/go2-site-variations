Local Company Discovers 35% of Work Hours Spent on Tasks That Could Be Automated

By Margaret Chen | Workflow Bureau Chief

Meridian Supply Co., a 45-person logistics company in Portland, made a startling discovery last month: more than a third of its workforce's productive hours were being consumed by repetitive tasks that could be partially or fully automated, according to data collected by a new desktop monitoring and coaching platform.

The finding came after the company deployed Go2's Cowork.ai desktop agent across its operations team. The agent, which sits quietly on each worker's computer and observes workflow patterns, compiled the data over a 14-day observation period without requiring any behavior changes from employees.

"We knew things were inefficient. We didn't know it was this bad."

-- Tom Hadley, COO, Meridian Supply Co.

"The thing that shocked me wasn't the 35% number," said Tom Hadley, Meridian's COO, in an interview. "It was what the 35% was made of. Data entry between systems. Copy-pasting customer information. Regenerating the same reports in slightly different formats. We had seven people doing work that, honestly, a well-configured AI agent could handle."

The discovery has reignited debate about the gap between AI's promise and its reality in small and mid-sized businesses. While enterprise AI spending topped $180 billion globally in 2025, studies consistently show that the vast majority of implementations fail to deliver measurable return on investment.

What made Meridian's experience different, analysts say, is the approach. Rather than attempting to deploy automation tools based on assumptions about how work should flow, Go2's platform first captures how work actually flows, then coaches workers toward better practices, and only then identifies automation opportunities grounded in real data.

"Most AI tools are answers looking for questions," said Dr. Sarah Kim, who studies AI implementation at Stanford's Human-AI Interaction Lab. "They say, 'Here's what our AI can do, now find a use case.' Go2 flips that. It says, 'Here's what your people actually do all day. Now let's figure out which parts an AI should handle.'"

Within 90 days of deployment, Meridian reported automating 11 workflows, reducing manual data entry by 78%, and achieving what Hadley described as "the first AI project that actually paid for itself, and then some."

The company declined to share exact financial figures but described the ROI as "over 300%," a number that, if accurate, would place it in the rarified top 5% of AI implementations that deliver positive returns within the first year.

Go2, founded in 2024, has attracted attention for its unconventional approach to a market littered with failed promises. The company's desktop agent doesn't require complex integrations, API connections, or months of setup. It installs on individual workstations and begins learning immediately.

"We're not selling a vision of AI," said Go2 in a statement. "We're selling proof. The data comes from your own workflows. The coaching comes from your own patterns. The automations come from your own processes. Nothing is generic."

MIT Study Confirms: 95% of Enterprise AI Fails to Deliver ROI

By David Park | Technology Correspondent

A comprehensive study released Tuesday by MIT's Sloan School of Management has confirmed what many suspected: 95% of enterprise AI implementations fail to deliver measurable return on investment within two years of deployment.

The study, which analyzed 2,400 AI projects across 14 industries, identified three primary failure modes: insufficient training data (cited in 67% of failures), lack of worker adoption (54%), and misalignment between AI capabilities and actual business processes (71%).

"The technology isn't the problem," said lead researcher Dr. James Liu. "The problem is deployment methodology. Companies are deploying AI the way they deployed software in the 1990s --- buy it, install it, hope people use it. That doesn't work with AI."

The study noted that the small percentage of successful implementations shared a common trait: they began with detailed observation and documentation of existing workflows before attempting any automation.

"The winners spent 60% of their implementation timeline just understanding how work actually happens," Dr. Liu noted. "The losers jumped straight to 'let's automate everything.'"

"Companies deploy AI the way they deployed software in the '90s. Buy it, install it, hope."

-- Dr. James Liu, MIT Sloan

The findings lend support to a new category of AI tools --- sometimes called "workflow-first AI" --- that prioritize process observation and worker coaching over immediate automation. Go2, a startup in this category, has reported customer ROI rates exceeding 300%, though independent verification of those figures is ongoing.

Why I Stopped Tracking My Team and Started Coaching Them

By Rachel Torres | Guest Columnist

Last year, I installed employee monitoring software on my team's computers. I told myself it was about "accountability" and "optimization." What I got was resentment, paranoia, and three resignations.

The monitoring tool showed me everything: keystrokes per minute, websites visited, application usage, idle time. It was surveillance dressed up as analytics. My team felt it. Morale cratered. The data told me people were "unproductive" but couldn't tell me why, or what to do about it.

Then a friend recommended Go2. I was skeptical --- another tool that watches people work? But the difference became clear immediately. Go2 doesn't report to managers. It coaches workers directly.

Instead of a dashboard telling me that Sarah spent 40 minutes on "unproductive" sites, Go2 showed Sarah herself that her invoice processing workflow had three redundant steps and offered to help streamline it. Instead of flagging Mike's "idle time," it noticed he was waiting on data from another system and suggested an automation.

"Monitoring tells you who's failing. Coaching tells you what's failing."

-- Rachel Torres

The philosophical difference is everything. Monitoring says, "We don't trust you." Coaching says, "We want to help you." One breeds fear. The other breeds improvement.

Six months in, my team is 35% more productive. Not because they're being watched. Because they're being helped. Three workflows are fully automated. Two AI agents handle tasks that used to eat up someone's entire Monday.

I threw away the monitoring software. I sleep better at night. And my team actually likes coming to work again.

Rachel Torres is COO at Brightpath Consulting, a 28-person firm in Austin, Texas.
